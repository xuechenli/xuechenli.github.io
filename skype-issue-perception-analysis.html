Skype Issue Perception Analysis

The Skype team had several channels for user feedback and product performance such as Net Promoter Score (NPS) survey and telemetry data dashboards. Although these channels provided a basic overview of existing issues, the team did not have the decision-making information to help them prioritize the issues to solve. With the help from the research team, an idea of learning users' perceptions of issues emerged. The team sent out four in-app surveys to users trying to understand which issues were more prevalent.

I was the only researcher that defined the analysis approach and carried it out.
Four in-app surveys were sent out to 60 thousand users. After the team finished collecting data, I took on the task of data analysis.
Survey 1: General Issue

asked if the user had any issues in the past 7 days

Survey 2: Feature Areas

asked if the user had any issues within Skype's feature areas (e.g., calling, messaging...)

Survey 3: Technical Areas

asked if the user had any issues within some technical areas (e.g., app crashes, audio quality issues...)

Survey 4: App Attributes

asked if the user was dissatisfied with some app attributes (e.g., ease of use, technical reliability...)

After the team finished collecting data, I took on the task of data analysis. To summarize the results of the 4 surveys, my first task was to clean the data.

The survey results were paired with respondents' recent telemetry data. I looked into the data of several activities to examine if a respondent was an active user of a certain function. Then I filtered out the unqualified entities.

The next task was to analyze the cleaned data. Aside from the 4 surveys, Skype had regular NPS surveys. So a small portion of the users had taken both the issue survey and NPS survey. This led to two analysis topics:

Issue Prevalence

The results of the 4 surveys provided how many people of the respondents had certain issues. The prevalence of each issue category could be found and compared from different aspects of survey 2, 3, and 4.

Issue-Detractor Relationship

Given a hypothesis that having issues would make one person be less happy about a product, I expected to find some relationship between users experiencing issues and they being NPS detractors (score below 6).

For the issue prevalence, it was an easy task to do descriptive statistic and to show it by bar charts.
For the issue-detractor relationship, I tried Chi-squared tests to see if experiencing a particular issue was significantly related to being a detractor; I tried Bayes' law to see the probability of being a detractor when having a particular issue; I also tried Person correlation to see if any issue was strongly correlated to being a detractor.

At last, I used the logistic regression model to calculate the significance and odds ratio of each issue with being a detractor. It turned out all issues were significantly related to being detractors. And the values of odds ratio in comparison provided a good aspect to see which issue was more likely to cause being a detractor.
Combining the prevalence and odds ratio, I drew 3 scatter plot for survey 2, 3, and 4. The more prevalent and higher odds ratio one issue is, the higher priority it should be.
My research results gave the team a straightforward insight into how vital each issue was and which one they should solve first.

In the presentation, I also reflected on how useful each survey was. As I proved the value of these surveys and suggested some moderation to the surveys, all of the team advocated the idea to run the issue prevalence surveys regularly, and even to promote this method to other products that the bigger organization owns.

This project was also a great learning experience for me. I had been discussing with a data scientist about different methods to use; I tried my first R script for making correlation plots. I look forward to more quantitative research studies in the future.
