Skype Core Problems Mapping

Most people use Skype for two things: calling and messaging.
While Skype has a good customer foundation and has kept evolving, the user experience of calling and messaging still suffered from a lot of inconsistent flows.The design initiated the conversation about tidying up the system. They identified that a comprehensive examination of the system was the key foundation.
As both the Skype system and the project itself were somewhat ambiguous, my goal was to disambiguate them:

Discover and mark down any issues that were lying in the calling and messaging flows.

Map and synthesize issues in a systemic way so that the issues are easy to look up.

Conduct heuristics evaluations on the issues and explore the solutions.

As I understood more about the problems and needs of my stakeholders over time, I realized that I also need to:

Tell stories from the user's aspect to engender empathy and to bring every partner onboard to act on the issues.

While the map was comprehensive, I was always aware that a map such big would be difficult to read. I found out a way to tell coherent stories while not losing details:

For discovering issues, I tested the calling and messaging under various circumstances. I took screenshots for each one of the flows and formed a giant flow map in Sketch.

While the map was comprehensive, I was always aware that a map such big would be difficult to read. I found out a way to tell coherent stories while not losing details:
1
Simple Scenarios

The giant map was broken down into 4 PowerPoint decks each of which contained a key scenario

2
Issue Summary

Each PowerPoint deck began with an overview of the scenario. It listed the amount of the issues and their categories.

3
Overview Map

Each PowerPoint slide zoomed into one or a few screenshots. Each slide had a little window that showed the relative position in the overview map.

4
Clear Flows

The deck was presented in one major flow. Other possibilities were kept as side flows that can be visited aside from the major flow. (Their screenshots were hidden in the overview for simplicity.)

In this project, I identified 56 issues that were categorized into inconsistent action/flow, unpredictable menu, hidden cost, faulty error state, inconsistent UI, and general UI issues. I was able to adjust and improve my deliverables across the time as I had kept regular communications with the design team. "This is exactly what we need!", said the design lead after listening to my walkthrough of the deck.
At the final presentation, we did a great job persuading the PM director to set about tackling the issues. My decks had given the team great confidence to achieve that result.
